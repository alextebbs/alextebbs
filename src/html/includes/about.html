About Me

If you are in the business of creating websites, you want someone on your team who is good with CSS. I'm incredible at writing CSS, and I've spent a lot of time refining a toolchain that allows me to write and iterate on CSS/HTML very quickly. This means I can produce live, interactive mockups just as easily as a traditional Photoshop designer produces a static PSD.

In the past I've worked with companies who have realized this, and turned their design process upside-down, stripping out their static mockup designer, and shipping projects directly to me for design in HTML. From there, I can work closely with purely developer-focused team members all the way to project launch.

I'm not suggesting I can replace an entire design team: I also work well as a liaison between design and development camps, translating requests from design-land into clear, actionable instructions for developers, or coming back to designers and other stakeholders to explain why their seemingly simple request might involve more developer time than expected.

* I really identify with the description of a "Frontend Designer" explained in this article by Brad Frost. Check it out. This isn't a position that exists at most companies today, but times are changing.

My History

I started out my career as a purely visual designer, working in print. A few aggressive mentors I've had taught me strong fundamentals of typography and layout (including the difference between em and en dashes). But print design wasn't for me, because one day, I fell in love with a language called CSS, and started creating websites. After freelancing for a year or two, I began working at various creative agencies. Every one of them has been very fast paced and deadline-focused. I'm used to switching projects on a weekly or daily basis, meaning the amount of websites I've had a hand in deploying is in the hundreds. This gives me a very wide breadth of experience with many different visual styles.

My Design Philosophy

I like to teach websites tricks. This site knows a few tricks. I want everything I do to be functional, but beyond that I have a more ethereal goal to pleasantly surprise the user. To make the user stop and think: "Woah, that was a little different." I hate out-of-the-box design and I can spot a $50 Wordpress theme before the page finishes rendering. I strive to try something new for each project, even if the ideas I create never make it live, experimentation is what helps me grow as a designer. I think analytics-based approaches to UX make sense at a large scale, but I also think there are UI patterns waiting to be discovered that don't show up in analytics or "best-practice" articles. I experiment in the hopes to discover and test these patterns.

My Tools

Beyond the basics (Yes I know HTML and CSS), here are some specific libraries and tools I use to get shit done.

Sass

I can't live without Sass. I prefer the indented syntax, but I can live with SCSS when needed. I've written my own Sass framework which includes plenty of helper mixins and functions that make my life easier when it comes to repeating patterns across multiple projects.

Javascript/jQuery

I typically use Javascript for controlling layout logic. I have some experience creating custom Javascript libraries, but I find most of my needs as a UI designer are served well by existing JS/jQuery plugins. I don't consider myself a Javascript developer, but I know my way around the language.

Ractive.js

Lipsum Dolor sit amet. I dont know Ractive but i want to use it to make this website. Once the website is done, I will write some text here about how I used ractive.js to help me make this website.

Terminal

I'm not scared of the command line. I use zsh because it has great out-of-the-box autocompletion support. I used to use iTerm 2 but decided most of my needs could be serviced by the humble Terminal.app.

Git

I use git for version control (suprise!). Typically I use the CLI as I find that to be the most expressive way of doing things.

Jade

I use Jade primarily as a way to save keystrokes when creating a static mockups. I like the clean syntax and ability to easily repeat a block of code with different data for the purposes of mocking up a large list.

Gulp

I often use gulp as a builder/watcher, primarily to compile Sass, but also to compress images and run other preprocessors like Jade if needed.

Vim

I fucking love Vim. Vim lets me move extremely fast, which helps greatly when iterating on a design. I've been using vim for about 5 years now and I don't think I can go back.

Adobe Creative Suite

I've been using Adobe products my entire career. These days I can get frustrated when spending too much time in Photoshop away from my beloved code, but I know how to get things done across the suite of Adobe visual design products, and still think they are integral tools to my workflow.
